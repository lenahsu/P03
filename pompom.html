Pompom

PomPom is an interactive system designed to aid children ages five to eight in musical and visual expression; the intent was to explore new opportunities and integrate TEI within educational tools to enhance the role of music in early children development.

Duration

This project was created over five weeks on my exchange semester at Malmö University.

Brief

For this tangible and embodied interaction course, the objective was to analyze existing educational tools and suggest new tangible and embodied interactions to incorporate into embodied learning.

I took this course during my exchange semester. What was unique about this project was that I got to experience different approaches and methods in interaction design while immersing myself in Swedish culture. Malmö University has given me the opportunity to freely explore and build on my foundation at my home university while offering a different perspective. My exchange semester has given me the opportunity to reflect on the connections I make around me and the impact of good design on different communities.

Opportunity

TUI for creative Expression

From the research compiled, it was evident that there was value in using music as a tool to learn something else as it can be integrated in all curricular areas to enhance creativity.

Much learning occurs without conscious awareness, suggesting that the user's secondary focus can be elsewhere while simultaneously interacting with music.

There is also alot of supporting evidence that tangible user interfaces can empower artsits, boost their creative expression, and enhance performing art.

User Experience

The animations play from and on the computer in real-time with sounds allowing children the freedom of prolonged tones and tempos with syncing animations. The user is free to explore and press on the controller to create their own audiovisual artwork.

Physical Artifact

The physical artifact was designed so that it invites interactions from any direction encouraging cooperation between several children. Using Dreyfuss' ergonomic design factors, the triangular shape of the controller and the cushion curves call for the action of pressing.

On-Screen Interface

The Seashell Sine's audiovisual mimics piano key frequencies because classical music notes are proven especially easy for children to adapt to.

The on-screen interface started out with abstract animations created on Adobe Illustrator to let children have the freedom to explore different shapes and styles.

User Testing

The user testing was conducted individually and collaboratively on two participants ages five and eight for 20 minutes through observations and interviews.

The participants had a need to physically relate to the boards and suggested landscapes with fishes and oceans instead. Heavily influenced by this, the design iterations following the user testing was changed to include environmental elements.

The participants' names and faces are not mentioned and are censored out due to underage policies.

Moving Forward

Second Layer
Customization

Moving forward from Pompom, a second layer interaction and a customizable feature would be required. To challenge the interaction and increase engagement, we would add more boards and combos where users can create new sounds and new animations with uploadable shareable content.

I had a lot of fun building Pompom with my team while fully delving into tangible and embodied interaction design. With them, I gained a lot of different insights into Malmö University's approaches on concept development, user research, and prototyping. We worked closely everyday for five weeks together and built off of one another's ideas. Our finish product resulted in a working prototype and a detailed paper on our extensive research, product, process, and further considerations.
